# Containment Protocol — Pressure Mechanics Spec

## Purpose

This document defines the pressure mechanics for Containment Protocol.

Pressure is the system that prevents the campaign from becoming a static optimization exercise.

It is how the game expresses:

- unresolved threat
- overload
- delay cost
- institutional strain
- faction response
- legitimacy exposure
- cascading consequences across weeks

Pressure is not just “difficulty.”
It is the bounded systemic force that pushes the agency into consequential tradeoffs.

This spec is for:

- system design
- implementation planning
- tuning
- UI surfacing
- test planning

---

## Design goals

Pressure mechanics should:

- create urgency without requiring real-time play
- force prioritization
- make delay and overextension matter
- connect missions, agency systems, hub state, and faction reaction
- remain deterministic and explainable
- support multiple forms of consequence beyond direct mission failure
- preserve campaign continuity

Pressure should not:

- feel like arbitrary punishment
- become a generic invisible difficulty scaler
- collapse all systems into one abstract stress meter
- remove player agency through unavoidable decay
- become so fragmented that the player cannot read what matters

---

## 1. What pressure is

Pressure is any bounded state that increases strategic difficulty or consequence burden over time or through overload.

Examples:

- unresolved incident escalation
- support strain
- coordination overload
- recovery backlog
- legitimacy exposure
- faction pressure
- rival pressure
- replacement pressure
- visibility pressure
- hub instability
- time pressure on specific missions

Pressure can be:

- local
- system-specific
- campaign-wide

The player should experience pressure as:

- something readable
- something caused
- something manageable but not ignorable

---

## 2. Pressure philosophy

The core pressure model should be:

```text
More unresolved problems
+ more simultaneous commitments
+ weaker support/recovery state
+ worse legitimacy or faction posture
= less clean institutional control
```

Pressure should emerge from:

- what the player did
- what the player delayed
- what the world is already carrying
- what capacities are currently saturated

Pressure should not emerge primarily from:

- invisible random spikes
- arbitrary weekly punishment
- flavor-only “the world feels worse” text

---

## 3. Pressure categories

Containment Protocol should use several pressure classes, each with a clear source and consequence path.

### 3.1 Incident pressure

Generated by:

- unresolved incidents
- delayed response
- escalation
- threat drift

Effects:

- incident worsening
- harder mission conditions
- additional follow-on incidents
- reduced clean containment windows

### 3.2 Support pressure

Generated by:

- too many concurrent operational demands
- insufficient support capacity
- follow-through burden
- recovery burden

Effects:

- degraded mission follow-through
- support shortages
- weaker week-to-week agency throughput

### 3.3 Specialist pressure

Generated by:

- specialist bottlenecks
- overcommitted recovery or capability lanes
- missing specialist coverage

Effects:

- reduced throughput
- delayed recovery
- blocked capability use
- more visible institutional bottlenecks

### 3.4 Coordination pressure

Generated by:

- concurrent load
- support strain
- too many simultaneous operations
- future handoff or overload conditions

Effects:

- degraded operational coherence
- weaker clean follow-through
- extra bounded friction in weekly execution

### 3.5 Recovery pressure

Generated by:

- injuries
- trauma
- damaged equipment
- insufficient specialist throughput
- repeated deployments without recovery

Effects:

- reduced readiness
- narrower routing options
- future capacity loss

### 3.6 Legitimacy pressure

Generated by:

- public visibility
- failed containment
- collateral spillover
- politically visible operations
- faction-sensitive mishandling

Effects:

- narrower access
- worse contracts/opportunities
- increased public or institutional resistance
- more fragile clean-response options

### 3.7 Faction pressure

Generated by:

- relationship deterioration
- rival activity
- contested space
- repeated interference or neglect

Effects:

- worse opportunity quality
- conflict or obstruction
- distorted hub signals
- increased strategic instability

### 3.8 Resource pressure

Generated by:

- funding shortfall
- procurement constraints
- upkeep load
- replacement burden

Effects:

- weaker loadouts
- delayed recovery
- stalled upgrades
- sharper tradeoffs

---

## 4. Core pressure loop

Pressure should follow a clear weekly loop:

Existing pressure
-> player triage and deployment
-> insufficient response / overload / delay
-> pressure increases or shifts
-> downstream systems worsen
-> next week starts under altered constraints

Pressure is one of the main sources of continuity between weeks.

---

## 5. Pressure sources

Pressure should come from identifiable sources.

### 5.1 Delay

If the player does not act, some problems worsen.

### 5.2 Overcommitment

If the player acts too broadly at once, institutional quality degrades.

### 5.3 Failure

If missions fail or partially succeed badly, pressure rises elsewhere.

### 5.4 Attrition

If the player burns through people, gear, and support, future flexibility drops.

### 5.5 Exposure

If visibility or legitimacy worsens, future options narrow.

### 5.6 Reactive world state

Factions, districts, and sites may change because of prior outcomes.

Pressure should never feel uncaused.

---

## 6. Pressure sinks / relief paths

A good pressure system must also define how pressure is reduced.

Pressure relief may come from:

- successful mission resolution
- support recovery
- specialist throughput
- facility upgrades
- funding/procurement improvements
- better intel/recon
- clean hub opportunities
- deliberate delay of expansion in order to stabilize

This is important:
pressure should be serious, but it must also be strategically manageable.

---

## 7. Pressure behavior rules

### Rule 1: Pressure must be surfaced

If the player is punished by pressure, the game must show:

- what the pressure is
- why it is active
- what it is affecting

### Rule 2: Pressure should be specific

Bad:

“stress increased”

Good:

“support strain degraded follow-through”

“district visibility increased legitimacy pressure”

### Rule 3: Pressure should have bounded effects

Do not create massive invisible all-stat penalties.
Prefer named, interpretable consequences.

### Rule 4: Pressure should create tradeoffs, not only punishment

Pressure should force choice:

- deploy now with strain
- wait and risk escalation
- spend resources here and accept weakness elsewhere

### Rule 5: Pressure should chain

Pressure is strongest when one burden creates another:

- delayed response -> escalation
- escalation -> harder mission
- harder mission -> partial success
- partial success -> legitimacy loss
- legitimacy loss -> worse future access

---

## 8. Pressure state model

Pressure can be represented conceptually as bounded flags, levels, or statuses.

Examples:

```ts
interface AgencyPressureState {
  supportStrain?: boolean;
  overload?: boolean;
  recoveryPressure?: boolean;
  legitimacyPressure?: boolean;
  replacementPressure?: boolean;
}

interface IncidentPressureState {
  escalationLevel: number;
  timeCritical: boolean;
  visibilityRisk?: boolean;
}
```

Prefer:

- bounded flags
- small enums
- low-complexity level bands

Avoid:

- giant hidden pressure vectors the player cannot interpret

---

## 9. Pressure triggers

Pressure should activate from explicit conditions.

Examples:

### Support strain — Trigger

Trigger:

support demand > support available

### Coordination overload — Trigger

Trigger:

active operation count + support strain > bounded threshold

### Recovery pressure — Trigger

Trigger:

unresolved recovery backlog exceeds weekly handling capacity

### Legitimacy pressure — Trigger

Trigger:

public visibility / collateral outcomes exceed acceptable range

### Faction pressure — Trigger

Trigger:

repeated conflict, disrespect, exposure, or contested access

These should be deterministic and inspectable.

---

## 10. Pressure consequences

Each pressure type should map to a small, real set of consequences.

Example consequence mapping

### Support strain — Consequence

- degrade mission follow-through
- surface support shortage notes

### Specialist bottleneck — Consequence

- delay recovery or throughput jobs
- surface explicit bottleneck notes

### Coordination overload — Consequence

- degrade one weekly operation output
- surface coordination friction note

### Legitimacy pressure — Consequence

- narrow clean response options
- worsen contract / access quality
- increase visibility-sensitive mission burden

### Faction pressure — Consequence

- distort opportunities
- reduce cooperation
- add interference risk

### Recovery pressure — Consequence

- reduce next-week readiness
- limit redeployment

---

## 11. Surfacing pressure

Pressure should appear in:

- status bar / pressure strip
- agency bottleneck summaries
- mission/deployment warnings
- weekly reports
- projections

A player should not need to guess why the campaign feels worse.

Examples of good surfaced text:

Support overload likely.

Maintenance bottleneck active.

District visibility rising.

Replacement pressure increased after recent losses.

Coordination overload reduced operation follow-through this week.

---

## 12. Pressure in the weekly loop

### Before deployment — Pressure loop

Pressure influences:

- which missions feel safe
- what bottlenecks are visible
- whether concurrent routing is wise

### During resolution — Pressure loop

Pressure may degrade:

- mission quality
- follow-through
- fallout containment
- clean withdrawal or recovery

### After resolution — Pressure loop

Pressure updates:

- agency state
- faction state
- hub state
- incident escalation
- readiness / recovery burden

---

## 13. Pressure and player psychology

The player should feel:

- constrained, but not trapped
- punished for overextension, not for existing
- rewarded for preparation and stabilization
- that ignoring weak signals now becomes bigger work later

Good pressure produces:

- difficult prioritization
- meaningful regrets
- visible strategic learning

Bad pressure produces:

- opaque frustration
- hopelessness
- “the game is just harder for no reason”

---

## 14. Tuning levers

Pressure tuning should be explicit.

Examples:

- unresolved incident escalation rate
- support strain threshold
- coordination overload threshold
- recovery backlog tolerance
- legitimacy damage threshold
- faction hostility escalation rate
- pressure decay / recovery speed
- number of simultaneous unresolved incidents tolerated safely

These should be tuned so the player is:

- usually under some pressure
- sometimes overloaded
- not permanently doomed unless play is consistently poor

---

## 15. Example pressure chain

Player routes too many operations
-> support strain activates
-> coordination overload triggers
-> one mission follow-through degrades
-> outcome becomes partial instead of clean
-> visibility rises
-> legitimacy pressure increases next week
-> future clean access narrows

This is a good pressure chain because:

- each step is caused
- each step is surfaced
- the player can learn from it

## 16. Anti-patterns

### Before deployment

Pressure influences:

- which missions feel safe
- what bottlenecks are visible
- whether concurrent routing is wise

### During resolution

Pressure may degrade:

- mission quality
- follow-through
- fallout containment
- clean withdrawal or recovery

### After resolution

Pressure updates:

- agency state
- faction state
- hub state
- incident escalation
- readiness / recovery burden

---

## 17. Anti-patterns

### Anti-pattern 1: Hidden difficulty inflation

Do not make pressure just a secret scaler.

### Anti-pattern 2: Single universal stress number

A single number can be useful in UI summary, but the systems still need distinct pressure causes.

### Anti-pattern 3: No recovery path

If pressure only rises and never meaningfully falls, strategic play becomes fatalistic.

### Anti-pattern 4: Over-splintering

Too many tiny pressure categories create unreadable complexity.

### Anti-pattern 5: Low signal surfacing

If the player sees “conditions worsened” instead of a real causal note, pressure loses learning value.

---

## 18. Testing expectations

Pressure tests should verify:

- triggers activate deterministically
- effects apply only when expected
- pressure chains propagate to downstream systems correctly
- pressure relief paths work
- surfaced output matches canonical domain state
- same state -> same pressure result

---

## 19. Acceptance criteria

Pressure mechanics are working when:

- incidents, overload, and neglect create visible strategic consequences
- the player can identify active bottlenecks and why they exist
- pressure changes future options and mission quality meaningfully
- relief paths exist and are strategically understandable
- reports and UI clearly explain important pressure states
- pressure remains deterministic and bounded

---

## 19. Summary

Pressure mechanics in Containment Protocol are the force that turns individual problems into campaign-level strategy.

Pressure should:

- emerge from state
- chain across systems
- remain visible
- create tradeoffs
- teach the player how the institution actually works

The player should feel:

every unresolved problem, overloaded lane, and degraded outcome is making the next week harder in a specific, understandable way.
